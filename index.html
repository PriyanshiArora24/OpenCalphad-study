01 Introduction to OpenCalphad

OpenCalphad (OC) is a free, open-source computational thermodynamics software for calculating phase equilibria and constructing phase diagrams in multicomponent alloy systems. It lets materials scientists predict which phases are stable under any given temperature, pressure, and composition, removing the need for costly trial-and-error experiments.

The CALPHAD Method

OpenCalphad is built on the CALPHAD method (CALculation of PHAse Diagrams), the internationally accepted framework for computational thermodynamics.

CALPHAD works by combining experimental data with thermodynamic models to build mathematical descriptions of the Gibbs energy for every phase in a system. Once these descriptions exist in a database, phase equilibria can be calculated for any composition or temperature, including complex multicomponent alloys that could never be fully mapped by experiment alone.

The key insight is extrapolation: a CALPHAD database built from well-studied binary and ternary systems can reliably predict behaviour in 5-, 6-, or even 10-component industrial alloys.

How OpenCalphad Works

OC determines equilibrium by finding the combination of phases with the lowest total Gibbs free energy. It reads Gibbs energy expressions for every phase from a .TDB thermodynamic database file, then uses a numerical minimiser to find the stable phase assemblage. The database is the model, and without it no calculation is possible.

Real-world Applications

  • Steel and iron alloy design (phase transformations, carbide stability)
  • Ni-superalloy and Al-alloy development
  • Solidification and casting simulation
  • Heat treatment optimisation
  • Corrosion, slag, and high-temperature chemistry

Limitations

  • Predicts equilibrium only, with no kinetic or diffusion information directly
  • Accuracy is entirely dependent on database quality
  • The CAE (graphical) interface makes basic tasks easier, but advanced usage still requires learning command-line inputs, which can take time

02 Introduction to Gnuplot

Gnuplot is a free, command-line plotting program widely used in scientific computing. OpenCalphad generates numerical output, such as tables of temperatures, compositions and phase fractions. Gnuplot takes that data and turns it into phase diagrams, property diagrams and other graphs, making results easier to interpret and share.

OpenCalphad CAE calls Gnuplot automatically whenever a plot is generated, as long as it is installed and linked correctly. Users can customise axis labels, line styles and legends through OC CAE's "Modified Gnuplot" output option. In short, Gnuplot is the visualisation layer that brings OpenCalphad's results to life.

Key point: Gnuplot is a separate program that must be installed alongside OC CAE. The installation guide below walks through this process step by step.

Examples

Example Analyses Using OpenCalphad

Worked examples demonstrating OpenCalphad CAE across single equilibrium, property diagrams, phase diagrams and solidification calculations.

Case 1-1

Single Equilibrium: Fe–C–Cr

Objective
To study which phases are stable and their fractions for an Fe–0.1 wt% C–1 wt% Cr alloy at 1000 K.
Plot
Phase fractions at T=1000 K, W(C)=0.001, W(Cr)=0.01
Conclusions
  • At 1000 K, the alloy is dominated by BCC-A2 (ferrite), constituting nearly the entire structure (~98.95%).
  • A small amount of alloy carbide M7C3 (~0.87%) is also present, along with a very minor fraction of cementite (~0.17%).
  • Even a small addition of chromium (~1%) promotes the formation of complex carbides rather than maintaining a simple solid solution.
Case 2-1

Property Diagram: Fe–C

Objective
To understand how phase fractions change with temperature for steels with different carbon contents (0.1 wt% C and 1 wt% C).
Plots
(a) Fe–1%C: Mole fraction of phase vs Temperature
(b) Fe–0.1%C: Carbon content in coexisting phases vs Temperature (K)
Conclusions
  • Plot (a) shows for Fe–1%C that BCC (ferrite) dominates at low temperatures, FCC (austenite) appears at intermediate temperatures, and liquid is stable above ~1760 K, with graphite appearing only near the melting point.
  • Plot (b) shows how carbon distributes between coexisting phases in Fe–0.1%C: the BCC phase holds very little carbon, while the liquid and FCC phases dissolve progressively more carbon as temperature increases.
  • Together the two plots confirm that carbon solubility is markedly higher in austenite (FCC) than in ferrite (BCC), which is the thermodynamic basis for carburising and quench-hardening heat treatments.
Case 3-1

Phase Diagram: Fe–C

Objective
To generate a temperature vs composition (T–X) diagram for the Fe–C binary system.
Plots
(a) Phase fractions vs Temperature at fixed W(C)=0.001
(b) Temperature vs Composition (w% C)
Conclusions
  • Plot (a) at fixed W(C)=0.001 shows that BCC-A2 (ferrite) is dominant at low temperatures, FCC-A1 (austenite) appears in the intermediate range (~1000–1200 K), and liquid forms above ~1800 K; a trace of graphite is present throughout.
  • Plot (b) reproduces the classic Fe–C phase diagram: the eutectic point (~1420 K, ~4.3 wt% C) and the eutectoid (~1000 K, ~0.76 wt% C) are clearly visible from the phase boundaries.
  • The significantly larger FCC region compared to BCC in the T-X diagram confirms that austenite can dissolve far more carbon, which underpins the entire basis of steel heat treatment.
Case 3-3

Ternary Phase Diagram: Fe–Cr–Ni

Objective
To study phase stability at constant temperatures (1000 K and 1200 K) and understand how chromium and nickel influence the phase diagram.
Plots
(a) Fe–Cr–Ni isothermal section at T=1000 K
(b) Fe–Cr–Ni isothermal section at T=1200 K
(c) Fe-axis projection at T=1200 K
Conclusions
  • Nickel stabilises austenite (FCC) while chromium stabilises ferrite (BCC); their balance controls the resulting phase.
  • The sigma (σ) phase region is clearly identified — a brittle intermetallic that is generally undesirable in engineering applications.
  • Tie-lines and phase regions reveal how elements distribute between phases, which is crucial for designing duplex stainless steels.
Case 3-5

Phase Diagram: Fe–C–Cr

Objective
To analyse phase stability of the Fe–C–Cr system with respect to composition of C and temperature.
Plot
Temperature vs W%(C) at W(Cr)=0.13
Conclusions
  • The diagram maps phase stability across the full range of carbon composition (0–2 wt% C) at a fixed Cr content of 13 wt%, showing how increasing C shifts the system from ferritic to austenitic and then into carbide-forming regions.
  • BCC-A2 (ferrite) is stable at low carbon contents; FCC-A1 (austenite) expands with increasing carbon, consistent with carbon being a strong austenite stabiliser.
  • Alloy carbides M7C3 and M23C6 appear at distinct carbon and temperature ranges — M23C6 at lower carbon/temperature and M7C3 at higher carbon content — which is critical for understanding carbide precipitation in Cr-bearing steels.
  • The invariant lines (marked) indicate temperatures where three phases coexist, defining the limits of heat treatment windows for this alloy composition.
